Module Overview

Launching into Cybersecurity introduced core technical, academic, and professional skills needed to progress through the MSc programme, with a focus on foundations such as security principles, research methods, collaboration, and reflective practice.

Level 7 15 credits

Key themes

  • Introduction to cybersecurity concepts, threats, and opportunities.
  • Critical reading, academic writing, and Harvard-style referencing.
  • Research methods and interpreting scholarly cybersecurity literature.
  • Collaboration in distributed teams using online tools and discussions.
  • Ethics, professional conduct, and legal dimensions of security work.
  • Developing a growth mindset and reflective practice for lifelong learning.

My focus in this module

I used this module to transition from previous operations/support roles into a more security-oriented mindset. The assessments helped me practise structured academic writing, critically engage with current cybersecurity issues, and reflect on my own skills, strengths, and gaps before moving into more technical modules.

Seminars: Live sessions across the term reinforced study skills, discussed assessment expectations, and gave space to ask questions about reading, writing style, and how to participate effectively in collaborative discussions.

Units 1‚Äì12

The module was delivered across 12 weekly units, combining core concepts, readings, activities, and assessment preparation.

Taught online, asynchronous with weekly milestones

Unit 1: Cybersecurity foundations and MSc expectations

Orientation to the programme; overview of cybersecurity as a discipline; introduction to VLE, reading lists, and the ePortfolio.

  • Understand how this module scaffolds later technical modules.
  • Recognise core security concepts (CIA triad, risk, threat, vulnerability).
  • Set up initial ePortfolio structure and personal study plan.

Unit 2: Study skills, critical reading and sources

Focus on finding and evaluating academic sources relevant to cybersecurity problems.

  • Differentiate between scholarly, professional, and popular sources.
  • Practise critical reading and note-taking approaches.
  • Begin building a small library of articles for use in assessments.

Unit 3: Academic writing and referencing

Structuring arguments, paraphrasing effectively, and applying UoEO Harvard referencing.

  • Write clear, cohesive paragraphs that answer a set question.
  • Reference correctly to avoid plagiarism and support claims.
  • Draft and refine short written responses to cybersecurity prompts.

Unit 4: Cybersecurity landscape and careers

Overview of industry roles, specialisms (e.g. SOC, pen-testing, GRC), and typical career paths.

  • Identify areas of cybersecurity that match my skills and interests.
  • Link MSc modules to potential career trajectories.
  • Reflect on skills to develop (e.g. scripting, cloud, incident response).

Unit 5: Ethics, law and professional conduct

Ethical frameworks, legal constraints, responsible disclosure, and professional codes of conduct.

  • Explain why ethics and law are central to security work.
  • Recognise boundaries between legal/illegal security activities.
  • Relate ethical guidelines to my own professional behaviour.

Unit 6: Research methods for cybersecurity

Introduction to qualitative and quantitative approaches in cybersecurity research.

  • Interpret research questions and methods in journal articles.
  • Understand sampling, data collection, and basic analysis ideas.
  • Use research findings to support arguments in assignments.

Unit 7: Collaborative work and online discussion

Focus on contributing to and moderating online academic discussions.

  • Participate constructively in discussion forums.
  • Respond to peers with evidence-based arguments, not just opinion.
  • Relate discussion work to formal assessment requirements.

Unit 8: Cybersecurity issues in practice

Application of earlier skills to case-study style cybersecurity scenarios.

  • Analyse basic security incidents using module concepts.
  • Identify human, technical and organisational contributing factors.
  • Propose realistic improvements and mitigations.

Unit 9: Preparing for summative assessments

Planning, outlining and aligning evidence for the upcoming written assessments.

  • Interpret assessment briefs and marking criteria.
  • Build structured plans with clear sections and arguments.
  • Integrate feedback from earlier formative work.

Unit 10: Drafting and redrafting

Emphasis on refining drafts, checking structure, flow and referencing before submission.

  • Apply self-review checklists to written work.
  • Improve clarity and coherence through editing.
  • Ensure academic integrity through correct citation and paraphrasing.

Unit 11: Reflective practice and professional development

Introduction to reflection models and how to use them for continuous improvement.

  • Use simple reflective frameworks (e.g. Gibbs) for learning reviews.
  • Identify strengths and areas for development as a cybersecurity learner.
  • Connect module learning to medium-term career goals.

Unit 12: Consolidation and looking ahead

Final unit pulling together skills from the module and relating them to upcoming technical modules such as Network Security and Secure Software Development.

  • Consolidate the study and research skills developed across units 1‚Äì11.
  • Review progress against the initial learning plan from Unit 1.
  • Capture key artefacts and reflections for this ePortfolio.

Academic engagement

Online discussion activity requiring an initial post and responses to peers on a cybersecurity topic set by the tutor.

  • Demonstrated ability to read and interpret set readings.
  • Practised building short, referenced arguments in a conversational format.
  • Engaged with differing viewpoints and supported positions with evidence.

Building on peer interaction

A second discussion-based task where I had to extend or challenge prior debates, while reflecting on how my understanding had changed over the module.

  • Showed development of ideas over time, not just a single snapshot.
  • Linked discussion threads to module readings and personal experience.
  • Practised concise, respectful argumentation online.

Linked units: Units 7‚Äì8 (discussion and applied issues), Unit 11 (reflection).

Assessment 3: Individual Essay

Structured academic writing

A written essay on a contemporary cybersecurity topic, requiring use of academic literature, argument structure, and Harvard referencing.

  • Planned and drafted a structured academic essay with clear sections.
  • Integrated multiple sources to support and contrast viewpoints.
  • Demonstrated correct use of in-text citations and reference list.

Reflection

How this foundation module shaped my approach to the rest of the MSc and my cybersecurity career path.

Personal and professional development

Launching into Cybersecurity was less about deep technical content and more about building the habits and mindset I would need for the rest of the programme. Coming from operations and customer-support work, I used this module to re-learn how to read critically, write academically, and think like a cybersecurity professional rather than just a user or support agent.

The discussion-based assessments were particularly valuable. They pushed me to move beyond ‚Äúgut feeling‚Äù and instead support points with references, while engaging with people who had very different backgrounds and experiences. That experience has carried over into later modules like Network Security and Secure Software Development, where the same discipline is needed to justify design choices and threat models.

The written essay and technical assessment gave me a safe space to practise the full workflow: reading, planning, drafting, citing, and polishing to a deadline. I also realised how important reflective practice is ‚Äì looking back on what worked, what did not, and where I need to focus next. That‚Äôs why this ePortfolio exists: to track my progress from this starting point through to more advanced modules and, ultimately, into a security role.

Looking back from later modules, this was the point where I committed to cybersecurity as a long-term path ‚Äì not just out of curiosity, but as a deliberate career move backed by structured learning and evidence of my development.
