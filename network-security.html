Module overview

Purpose and Learning Outcomes

The module focused on the impact of cybersecurity on network systems and designs, with particular emphasis on the Vulnerability Assessment Continuum and the disciplined use of scanning, evaluation, monitoring, logging, and mitigation techniques.

01

Threat and Vulnerability Analysis

Identify and analyse threats and vulnerabilities in network systems, then select appropriate methodologies, tools, and techniques to investigate or manage them.

02

Risk-Aware System Design

Critically appraise systems and technical solutions that support security auditing, risk management, and responsible mitigation.

03

Evidence Synthesis

Gather and synthesise evidence from multiple sources, including technical scans, tool documentation, standards, and security advisories.

04

Professional Responsibility

Articulate the legal, social, ethical, and professional issues faced by information security and risk practitioners.

Core reading: Kaufman, Perlman and Speciner’s Network Security: Private Communications in a Public World supported the module’s treatment of network protocols, architectures, security controls, and communications in exposed environments.

Unit-by-unit development

Units 1–6

Each unit advanced from foundational network-security concepts toward practical assessment, breach response, monitoring, and emerging technologies.

Unit 1

History of Network Security, Vulnerabilities and Approaches

This unit established how network security evolved alongside increasingly connected systems. I considered how threats, vulnerabilities, risk, and defence-in-depth relate to one another, and why assessments must consider people, processes, protocols, architecture, and technology rather than isolated flaws.

Unit 2

Advanced Persistent Threats and the Cyber Kill Chain

Applying the Cyber Kill Chain to a case study helped me understand the staged nature of advanced attacks—from reconnaissance and weaponisation through delivery, exploitation, installation, command and control, and actions on objectives. The model also demonstrated where defensive controls can interrupt an intrusion.

Unit 3

Vulnerability Assessments

I planned a scoped, remote, and non-destructive assessment of demo.testfire.net. The baseline report documented likely security challenges, applicable standards, selected tools, operational impacts, assumptions, limitations, and a phased testing timeline.

Unit 4

Breach Analysis and Mitigation

This unit shifted the focus from discovering weaknesses to interpreting their possible consequences. I learned to connect technical evidence with business impact, prioritise remediation, and distinguish immediate controls from longer-term architectural improvements.

Unit 5

Logging, Forensics and Future Trends

I explored the importance of monitoring and logging for detecting abnormal activity, reconstructing incidents, supporting forensic investigation, and evidencing compliance. This also highlighted the value of SIEM platforms and reliable audit trails.

Unit 6

The Future of the Internet and Generative AI

The final unit considered how changing internet architectures and generative AI affect both attackers and defenders. It reinforced the need for continuous learning, careful validation of automated outputs, and security controls that can adapt to rapidly evolving technologies.

Practical investigation

Assessment Methodology and Tools

Testing was conducted from Kali Linux in VMware Fusion Pro against the deliberately vulnerable Altoro Mutual demonstration environment. Automated discovery was combined with targeted manual validation.

Nmap

Used for network mapping, service detection, open-port discovery, OS fingerprinting, and traceroute analysis. It exposed web services and atypical SMB ports that increased the visible attack surface.

Nikto

Used to identify web-server misconfiguration, outdated components, missing HTTP security headers, and unsafe responses to malicious input patterns.

OWASP ZAP

Provided dynamic application testing for reflected and DOM-based XSS, missing CSRF protections, insecure cookie attributes, and absent browser-security headers.

Burp Suite

Supported manual proxy-based testing of authentication, session tokens, HTTP responses, input handling, and exposed application endpoints.

SSLyze

Assessed TLS and certificate configuration, identifying deprecated protocol support and certificate weaknesses that affected secure transmission and compliance.

WHOIS and DIG

Supported target validation, ownership research, DNS resolution, infrastructure awareness, scoping, and responsible reporting.

Ethical boundaries: denial-of-service testing, brute-force attacks, destructive payloads, deep role enumeration, and privilege escalation were deliberately excluded. This preserved availability and kept the work within the permitted assessment scope.

Evidence and interpretation

Principal Findings and Compliance Mapping

The assessment moved beyond application-level OWASP issues to include network exposure, encryption, DNS, authentication, session control, logging, and regulatory risk.

Technical Findings

  • Weak credentials without effective rate limiting.
  • Reflected and DOM-based cross-site scripting.
  • Missing CSRF tokens and weak session controls.
  • Missing CSP, X-Frame-Options, and other security headers.
  • Outdated Apache/Tomcat-related components.
  • Deprecated TLS support and certificate weaknesses.
  • Public exposure of SMB ports 139 and 445.
  • Missing DNS email-security protections such as SPF/DMARC.

Standards and Business Relevance

  • GDPR Articles 25 and 32: secure design, encryption, integrity, and resilience.
  • PCI DSS Requirement 1: restricted and securely configured network exposure.
  • PCI DSS Requirements 4 and 6: protected transmission and secure development.
  • PCI DSS Requirement 8: strong authentication controls.
  • PCI DSS Requirement 10: effective logging, monitoring, and alerting.
  • OWASP Top 10: misconfiguration, outdated components, authentication failures, and insufficient logging.
Prioritised response: the executive summary recommended immediate attention to CSRF protection, TLS configuration, server patching, security headers, session handling, and unnecessary network exposure. Medium- and lower-priority improvements were to follow within a defined remediation window.

Critical reflection

What Changed Through the Module

The most significant development was learning that useful security work depends on interpretation, context, prioritisation, and ethical judgement—not simply running tools or listing scan results.

What?

The first assessment concentrated heavily on OWASP application issues. Tutor feedback identified the need to consider system, middleware, network, and architectural weaknesses as well. The later assessment therefore adopted a layered toolset and broader compliance perspective.

So What?

I learned that a missing header, deprecated TLS version, exposed port, or weak session is meaningful only when its technical, business, legal, ethical, and user-safety implications are explained. This shifted my work from checklist reporting toward professional risk analysis.

Now What?

I plan to deepen my knowledge of TLS, certificate management, enterprise encryption, SIEM platforms such as Splunk, threat intelligence, and practical CTF exercises while continuing to respect legal scope and responsible-testing boundaries.
