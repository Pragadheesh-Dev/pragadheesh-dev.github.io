Module overview

Purpose of this ePortfolio

This module connects technical cybersecurity practice with law, evidence, policing, and forensic investigation. Earlier modules focused more on prevention, secure development, risk management, and architecture. This module shifts attention to what happens after an incident: how an event is investigated, how evidence is collected and interpreted, and how technical findings become meaningful in legal or organisational decision-making.

My ePortfolio therefore records more than unit completion. It shows the activities I engaged with, the concepts I learned, the artefacts I produced, and how my thinking developed as I moved through the first seven units.

Core learning themes
  • Law, legal systems, and cybercrime.
  • Evidence, proof, and admissibility.
  • Digital evidence identification and preservation.
  • Computer forensic investigation process.
  • Privacy, surveillance, and legal accountability.

Unit 1

The Law and Legal Systems

Foundations of law and cybercrime

Focus: legal systems, purpose of law, crime, and the legal context of cyber incidents.

Completed
Activities completed
  • Completed the Unit 1 lecturecast on law, legal systems, and cybercrime foundations.
  • Reviewed the introductory material on common law, civil law, and mixed legal systems.
  • Reflected on what law is, why societies need legal systems, and how law defines responsibility.
  • Prepared notes on how legal systems influence the investigation and prosecution of cybercrime.
What I learned

I learned that law is not simply a list of rules. It is a framework that defines rights, duties, prohibited conduct, procedures, enforcement powers, and accountability. This matters in cybersecurity because a technical event only becomes legally relevant when it can be connected to a legal definition, a responsible actor, a victim, harm, intent, and admissible evidence.

The unit also helped me understand that legal systems differ between jurisdictions. Common law systems rely heavily on precedent and judicial decisions, while civil law systems rely more on codified rules. Mixed systems combine more than one tradition. This is important because cybercrime often crosses borders, but laws and enforcement powers remain tied to national legal systems.

Reflection

Before this unit, I mainly viewed cybersecurity as a technical discipline. Unit 1 helped me recognise that cybersecurity professionals also need legal awareness. Technical findings must be documented clearly and explained in a way that supports organisational, regulatory, or legal decisions. This changed my perspective because it showed that investigation is not only about finding what happened, but also about showing why the finding is reliable and legally meaningful.

Unit 2

Principles of Evidence and Proof

Evidence, burden of proof, and legal standards

Focus: how evidence supports legal claims and how proof differs between legal contexts.

Completed
Activities completed
  • Studied the lecture material on evidence, burden of proof, and standards of proof.
  • Reviewed how criminal and civil contexts apply different evidential thresholds.
  • Considered how cyber evidence can create challenges for investigators and courts.
  • Started engagement with the formative discussion topic on cybercrime and organised crime.
What I learned

This unit helped me understand that evidence must do more than exist. It must be relevant, reliable, and capable of supporting a claim. I learned that the burden of proof refers to who must prove a claim, while the standard of proof refers to how convincing the evidence must be.

I also learned that digital evidence creates specific difficulties. Logs, timestamps, IP addresses, messages, device artefacts, and metadata can be useful, but they can also be incomplete, misunderstood, altered, or challenged. This means investigators must handle digital evidence carefully and explain it clearly.

Reflection

The most important learning point for me was that digital evidence does not automatically prove responsibility. A login record, IP address, or recovered file may suggest activity, but investigators still need to connect the evidence to a person, action, time, and context. This is especially relevant to SOC and blue-team work because alerts and logs may begin an investigation, but they are not always enough to reach a defensible conclusion.

Unit 3

What is Evidence Anyway?

Digital evidence and investigative meaning

Focus: identifying evidence, linking evidence to events, and maintaining continuity.

Completed
Activities completed
  • Completed the Unit 3 lecturecast on the meaning and role of evidence.
  • Reviewed guidance on identifying digital evidence and linking suspects to actions.
  • Worked on Activity 2: Computer Forensics - The Investigative Process.
  • Reflected on Walden's argument and considered the connection between law and policing through evidence.
  • Engaged with Bryant and Kennedy to consider how investigative processes have changed.
What I learned

Unit 3 helped me understand that evidence is not only information collected during an investigation. It becomes evidence when it is relevant to a question being investigated and can support or challenge a claim. In digital forensics, this can include device data, logs, communications, metadata, file activity, cloud records, and other digital traces.

I also learned that evidence must be placed into an investigative narrative. It is not enough to say that something was found on a device. The investigator must explain how it was found, why it matters, how it was preserved, and how it links to an action, person, or event.

Reflection

This unit made me think more carefully about the phrase "digital footprint". In everyday use, it sounds simple, but in a forensic context each trace must be interpreted carefully. A digital trace may indicate activity, but it may not automatically prove who performed it or whether the action was intentional. This helped me appreciate why continuity of evidence, documentation, and tool reliability are central to forensic investigation.

Unit 4

Computer Forensics and the Investigative Process

Investigation, policing, and evidence handling

Focus: structured forensic process and the relationship between law, policing, and evidence.

Completed
Activities completed
  • Prepared to discuss Activity 2 findings in Seminar 2.
  • Reviewed the role of investigative models in digital forensics.
  • Continued the collaborative discussion on cybercrime and organised crime.
  • Responded to peer contributions and considered different country perspectives.
What I learned

This unit strengthened my understanding of digital forensics as a process rather than a single technical task. Investigators must identify, preserve, collect, examine, analyse, and present evidence in a way that maintains integrity and can be reviewed later.

I learned that law and policing are connected through evidence. Police powers, investigative procedures, search and seizure rules, and evidential standards all influence what investigators can do and how their findings can be used. In cybercrime cases, this connection becomes more complicated because data may be stored across devices, services, and jurisdictions.

Reflection

This unit was useful because it made the investigative process feel more structured. In cybersecurity practice, it can be tempting to focus only on technical discovery, such as identifying malware, suspicious logins, or network traffic. However, forensic investigation requires a disciplined approach where every action can be explained. I realised that poor documentation or weak handling can reduce the value of otherwise useful evidence.

Unit 5

Digital Evidence, Cybercrime, and Organised Crime

Cyber-enabled crime and investigative challenges

Focus: organised crime, transnational activity, digital traces, and policing challenges.

Completed
Activities completed
  • Developed the formative discussion work on cybercrime and organised crime.
  • Analysed how organised criminal groups use digital tools to expand traditional crime.
  • Considered policing challenges created by encryption, anonymity, international cooperation, and fast-moving technologies.
  • Began shaping the EncroChat presentation topic around cyber-enabled organised drug trafficking in the United Kingdom.
What I learned

This unit helped me understand that not all cyber-related crime is purely cybercrime. Some offences, such as drug trafficking, remain traditional crimes, but they can become cyber-enabled when offenders use encrypted communication, digital payment methods, online coordination, or technical tools to organise activity.

I learned that organised criminal groups can use technology to increase scale, reduce visibility, coordinate across borders, and frustrate law enforcement. This creates a need for investigators who understand both criminal behaviour and digital evidence.

Reflection

This unit directly influenced my presentation topic. I realised that EncroChat was a strong example because the underlying offence was not simply hacking or computer misuse. It involved the use of digital forensic methods and legal cooperation to investigate cyber-enabled organised drug trafficking. This helped me frame the topic more critically instead of treating all technology-related offending as the same type of cybercrime.

Unit 6

Mid-Module Assignment 1: Presentation

EncroChat hacking and UK organised drug trafficking investigations

Summative artefact: presentation for Mid-Module Assignment 1.

Completed
Assessment focus

For the Unit 6 assessment, I prepared a presentation titled EncroChat Hacking and its Impact on Investigating Cyber-Enabled Organised Drug Trafficking in the United Kingdom. The presentation examined how the infiltration of EncroChat supported law enforcement investigations, especially the UK response through Operation Venetic.

Activities completed
  • Selected and refined the presentation topic.
  • Researched EncroChat, Europol cooperation, the UK National Crime Agency, and Operation Venetic.
  • Prepared a structured slide deck covering background, investigation, legal debate, court treatment, public perception, and critical evaluation.
  • Developed a teleprompter-style speaking script for recording.
  • Connected the topic to digital evidence, admissibility, privacy, surveillance, and cyber-enabled organised crime.
What I learned

This assessment helped me understand how a digital forensic investigation can have major operational and legal consequences. EncroChat showed how encrypted communication platforms can be used by organised criminal groups, but it also raised important questions about hacking by law enforcement, evidence collection, privacy, and admissibility.

I also learned the importance of careful framing. Drug trafficking itself is not normally a cybercrime, but the use of encrypted communication made it a cyber-enabled organised crime issue. This distinction helped me present the topic more accurately.

Reflection

Preparing this presentation improved my ability to connect technical investigation with legal analysis. I had to avoid treating the case as only a technical success story. A balanced view also required considering privacy, proportionality, human rights, and the risk of normalising intrusive investigative techniques. This helped me develop a more critical approach to digital forensics and cyber law.

Unit 7

Post-Presentation Development and Legal Reflection

Consolidating digital forensics, law, and evidence

Focus: continuing reflection after the first summative assessment and preparing for later portfolio work.

Completed
Activities completed
  • Reviewed the learning gained from the EncroChat presentation.
  • Continued building the ePortfolio page to record module progress and evidence of learning.
  • Prepared to move towards later work involving blog-style reflection and expert report development.
  • Reflected on how legal, ethical, and evidential issues affect forensic investigation outcomes.
What I learned

Unit 7 helped me consolidate the first half of the module. I learned that digital forensic work sits between technical investigation and legal interpretation. The technical side may identify artefacts, patterns, or activity, but the legal side asks whether the evidence is reliable, admissible, proportionate, and connected to the issue being investigated.

I also learned that cyber law requires a balance between public safety and individual rights. Investigative success does not automatically remove the need to question legality, privacy, proportionality, and oversight.

Reflection

By this stage of the module, my understanding has moved beyond simple definitions of law and evidence. I now see digital forensics as a disciplined process that must protect both investigative value and legal fairness. This is especially important for my future cybersecurity career because SOC and incident response work can generate evidence that may later support internal investigations, regulatory reporting, or law enforcement involvement.

Overall reflection

Learning development up to Unit 7

Completing Units 1-7 has changed how I understand the relationship between cybersecurity, law, and investigation. Earlier in my cybersecurity learning, I focused more on tools, attacks, systems, and controls. This module has shown me that investigation also depends on legal definitions, evidence handling, documentation, admissibility, and the ability to explain technical findings clearly.

The most important development in my thinking is that digital evidence must be treated carefully from the beginning. If evidence is not preserved properly, documented accurately, or interpreted cautiously, it may lose value even if it appears technically useful. This applies not only to criminal investigations, but also to SOC analysis, incident response, internal investigations, compliance reporting, and organisational decision-making.

The EncroChat presentation helped me connect theory to a real-world case. It showed that cyber-enabled organised crime creates serious policing challenges, but it also raised questions about privacy, surveillance, proportionality, and fairness. This balance is central to cyber law because effective investigation must still remain accountable.

Going forward, I want to continue developing the ability to write clearly about forensic findings, legal issues, and investigative reasoning. This will help me prepare for the blog post and final expert report, while also supporting my wider career goal of moving into defensive cybersecurity and SOC-related work.

Module Overview

Law meets cyber investigation

This module introduces the relationship between digital forensics and cyber law, focusing on how cyber incidents are investigated, interpreted, and handled within legal systems.

Unlike modules that focus mainly on prevention or secure system design, this module shifts attention to what happens after an incident: how evidence is identified, preserved, analysed, and presented in a legally meaningful way.

Core themes
  • Legal systems and how they define crime, responsibility, and enforcement.
  • Cybercrime as a legal and technical challenge.
  • Digital evidence handling, admissibility, and investigative reliability.
  • The difficulty of applying traditional legal concepts to fast-moving cyber environments.

The module is especially useful because it connects technical cybersecurity practice with accountability, evidence, and legal reasoning.

Unit 1: The Law and Legal Systems

Completed
Focus: Law, legal systems, cybercrime foundations Mode: Lecturecast + reading

Unit 1 introduced the basic idea of law and legal systems, asking what law is and why it matters in the context of cybercrime. The unit explained that law is not only a set of rules, but also a framework for defining rights, duties, responsibilities, offences, and enforcement.

A key part of the unit was understanding that legal systems differ across jurisdictions. England and Wales are typically associated with a common law system, while many European countries follow civil law traditions. Some jurisdictions, such as South Africa, operate mixed systems influenced by both common law and civil law principles.

Legal systems introduced
  • Common law – shaped strongly by judicial decisions and precedent.
  • Civil law – based mainly on codified statutes and legal codes.
  • Mixed legal systems – influenced by more than one legal tradition.

The unit also introduced the concept of crime and laid the foundation for understanding cybercrime. This is important because cybercrime cannot be evaluated purely through technical evidence; it must also be interpreted through legal definitions, jurisdiction, intent, harm, and admissibility.

Key takeaways
  • Cybersecurity incidents become legally meaningful only when connected to legal definitions and evidence.
  • Different legal systems may approach cybercrime, responsibility, and enforcement differently.
  • Cyber law must respond to fast-changing technology, cross-border activity, and digital evidence challenges.
  • Digital forensics acts as a bridge between technical investigation and legal accountability.
Reflection

This unit changed my perspective by showing that cybersecurity is not only about defending systems. It is also about understanding how incidents are investigated, documented, and assessed within legal systems. For a security professional, technical findings must be reliable, explainable, and suitable for legal or organisational decision-making.

Early Module Reflection

Initial understanding

At this early stage, the module has already expanded my understanding of cybersecurity beyond prevention, detection, and technical controls. It shows that cyber incidents must also be viewed through legal systems, evidence standards, and investigative processes.

Unit 1 helped me understand that legal context matters because cybercrime often crosses borders, involves different jurisdictions, and depends on how evidence is collected and interpreted. A technical finding is not enough on its own unless it can be documented and explained clearly.

This is relevant to my blue-team and SOC development because incident analysis often produces evidence such as logs, alerts, IP addresses, timestamps, authentication records, and endpoint activity. Digital forensics helps ensure that such evidence is handled carefully and remains useful for investigation, escalation, and possible legal action.
