Units 1 - 6

Learning snapshots and artefacts

Unit 1: Introduction to Operating Systems and Secure Systems Architecture

Foundations
Focus: OS fundamentals, process isolation, secure design Mode: Lecturecast + core reading

Unit 1 set the conceptual foundation for the module by revisiting how operating systems manage resources (CPU, memory, storage, I/O) and how these mechanisms influence security. This included process and thread models, scheduling, virtual memory, and how isolation boundaries underpin secure architectures.

At an architectural level, the unit contrasted monolithic kernels, microkernels, modular designs and cloud-native stacks, and discussed how design choices affect attack surface and resilience. This directly feeds into later units on distributed systems and formal modelling.

Key takeaways
  • Security requirements must be designed into OS-level abstractions (processes, users, permissions) rather than bolted on later.
  • Threats such as privilege escalation, TOCTOU, and insecure IPC often exploit weak boundaries between components.
  • Secure systems architecture sits at the intersection of OS design, networking, and formal reasoning about behaviour.
Representative artefacts
  • Concept map linking OS components (scheduler, MMU, filesystem) to typical threat categories.
  • Short write-up comparing monolithic vs microkernel designs from a security perspective.

Unit 2: Modelling and Socket Programming

Networked systems
Focus: Threat modelling and basic client-server sockets Seminar highlight: Attack-defense trees using ADTool

Alongside practical socket programming, the seminar introduced attack-defense trees and the ADTool as a way of modelling security scenarios for networked systems. The worked example used a small home Wi-Fi network with laptops and a smart TV.

The attacker's root goal was defined as compromising the home network. This was then decomposed into child goals such as compromising the router, the smart TV, or a laptop. Each attack path was expanded into specific techniques (weak Wi-Fi passwords, default credentials, malware via USB), and paired with explicit defender countermeasures (WPA3, changing default passwords, disabling autorun, antivirus).

Seminar - ADTool and quantitative attributes
  • Built an attack-defense tree in ADTool from scratch, defining the root goal ‚Äúcompromise network‚Äù and child nodes.
  • Used AND/OR operators to express whether multiple conditions must hold or whether any one path is sufficient for compromise.
  • Attached quantitative attributes such as probability of success, cost, time to attack, and skill levels for attacker and defender.
  • Explored how strengthening countermeasures (for example, WPA3 and disabling autorun) reduced the overall compromise probability for the network.
Representative artefacts
  • ADTool model of a home Wi-Fi network, including attacker goals and defender nodes.
  • Short note describing how probability assignments at the leaves propagate to an overall network compromise likelihood.

Unit 3: Systems Engineering and Modelling

Team project context
Focus: Requirements, SysML/UML, and architectural models Assessment link: Development Team Project

Unit 3 brought systems engineering discipline into the module. It focused on translating stakeholder requirements into structured models (for example, context diagrams, use-case diagrams, logical component views) and tracing these into secure design decisions.

The team project used these techniques to define the architecture of a secure system, document assumptions and threats, and reason about how design choices influence risk.

Key themes
  • Requirements engineering and acceptance criteria for secure systems.
  • Trade-offs between usability, performance, and security in architecture decisions.
  • Using diagrams and models to support both technical reasoning and non-technical communication.
Representative artefacts
  • Team architectural diagrams and narrative from Assessment 1 - Development Team Project.

Unit 4: Modelling of Distributed Systems

QoS and scientific framing
Focus: QoS metrics, NGN traffic engineering, teleological IS design Seminar highlight: ‚ÄúIs cybersecurity a science?‚Äù

The Unit 4 seminar centred on two core readings: a paper on Quality of Service performance analysis for next-generation wireless sensor networks, and a paper on a teleological approach to information system design.

QoS in next-generation networks
  • Defined QoS in terms of availability, bandwidth, latency, jitter, and packet loss for real-time traffic.
  • Discussed challenges from IoT scale, multimedia traffic, and constrained bandwidth.
  • Reviewed existing models such as best effort, IntServ (resource reservation) and DiffServ (differentiated services).
  • Summarised the proposed edge-based model that classifies traffic into Assured Forwarding (video), Expedited Forwarding (voice) and Best Effort (data), using weighted round-robin scheduling and adaptive buffers.
Teleological approach & explainability
  • Introduced teleosemantics and the idea that system design should be grounded in purpose and function.
  • Defined the four elements: producer (designer), produced artefact (system), consumer (user), and purpose (use goal).
  • Connected the framework to reliability, explainability, accountability and explainable AI in security-critical systems.
Seminar discussion: Is cybersecurity a science?
  • Used the Science Council definition of science as evidence-based, systematic, and repeatable.
  • Mapped scientific characteristics (observation, experimentation, induction, repetition, critical analysis, verification) onto cybersecurity practices.
  • Reflected on how penetration testing, incident response, and secure coding can follow a scientific method, while still leaving space for human creativity and ‚Äúart‚Äù.
Representative artefacts
  • Short summary contrasting IntServ, DiffServ and the proposed QoS model for NGNs.
  • Notes on how teleological design principles could improve transparency in future secure architectures.

Unit 5: Current and Future Challenges of Operating Systems and Distributed Systems

Trends & challenges
Focus: Emerging OS / distributed systems threats Preparation: Feeding into formal vs automated testing debate

Unit 5 looked ahead at the evolving landscape of operating systems and distributed systems: containerisation, serverless workloads, edge computing, ubiquitous IoT, and the complexity of heterogeneous platforms.

The unit linked these trends to new attack surfaces (for example, supply chain compromise, multi-tenant isolation failures, configuration sprawl) and to the need for stronger assurance methods, which then set up the Unit 6 debate on formal modelling and automated testing.

Key themes
  • Complexity as a security risk in multi-layered architectures.
  • Observability, logging, and monitoring as essential components of secure distributed design.
  • Where traditional OS security models struggle in highly distributed, ephemeral environments.
Representative artefacts
  • Short note comparing on-prem OS security concerns with cloud-native container and serverless deployments.
  • Threat list for an example microservices system, mapped to OS and network layers.

Unit 6: Formal Modelling vs. Automated Testing

Assurance methods
Focus: Formal methods, automated testing and trade-offs Seminar highlight: Debate framing + assignment guidance

The final seminar for the module framed a structured comparison between formal modelling and automated testing, drawing directly on the Unit 6 content and on the individual project work.

Formal modelling
  • Defined as the use of rigorous mathematical models to specify and verify software systems (Jones, 1986).
  • Key components:
    • Formal specification ‚Äì using mathematics to capture system requirements and desired properties.
    • Formal verification ‚Äì proving that an implementation satisfies those specifications.
    • Automated program generation ‚Äì generating code from mathematical models in some approaches.
  • Advantages:
    • Eliminates ambiguity in requirements and specifications.
    • Enables proofs of correctness for critical properties.
    • Forces deeper understanding of system inputs, outputs and state space.
  • Limitations: high complexity and expertise required, scalability challenges, and cost that is usually only justified for safety or mission-critical systems.
Automated testing
  • Uses scripts and tools to automatically execute test cases that a human tester would otherwise perform manually.
  • Motivated by high-profile failures where insufficient testing led to serious incidents (for example, aviation and infrastructure failures).
  • Benefits:
    • Supports rapid regression testing and higher test coverage.
    • Reduces human error and frees developer time for design and review.
    • Enables frequent, repeatable tests integrated into CI/CD pipelines.
  • Limitations: cannot fully automate all test types, cannot guarantee 100 per cent coverage, and may produce false positives or false negatives.
Seminar perspective and assignment link
  • The seminar emphasised that formal modelling and automated testing are complementary, not mutually exclusive.
  • Formal methods are powerful but expensive; automated testing is pragmatic and essential for fast-moving projects.
  • The individual project applied these ideas in practice through code, vulnerability analysis, and documentation capped at 1,300 words.
Representative artefacts
  • Discussion notes weighing formal proofs vs. extensive automated testing for different system criticality levels.
  • Code, test outputs, and documentation from the individual project in Assessment 2.

Module Reflection

From operating systems to assured architectures

Across Units 1 to 6, the Secure Systems Architecture module linked low-level operating system details with high-level architectural decisions, distributed systems behaviour, and formal assurance methods. The seminar content on ADTool, QoS, teleological design and formal modelling tied theory directly to practice in the assessments.

The progression from understanding OS internals, through modelling and threat analysis, to thinking about scientific method and proof, has reinforced the idea that secure architecture is not a single tool or diagram. It is a way of thinking: being explicit about assumptions, modelling adversaries, gathering evidence and choosing the right mix of formal methods and pragmatic automated testing for each system's risk profile.

The three assessments ‚Äì team project, individual project and reflective piece ‚Äì created a full loop: applying concepts in a group setting, demonstrating individual technical capability, and then critically reflecting on the entire journey. This ePortfolio page collects those elements in a single place for future reference and for building on in later modules.
