Case Study: Alleged disclosure of CCTV footage from a State-run accommodation centre (2018). The incident involved the unauthorised sharing of identifiable video data, prompting an investigation by the Irish Data Protection Commission (DPC).
üìò View Case Study Analysis

Specific GDPR Articles and Issues

The DPC determined breaches under the Data Protection Acts 1988 & 2003 (now aligned with the GDPR), highlighting failures to maintain lawful processing and adequate safeguards:

  • Section 4: No timely response to the subject‚Äôs access request (exceeding 40 days).
  • Section 2(1)(d): Insufficient security measures to prevent unauthorised disclosure.
  • Sections 2C(2) & 2C(3): Absence of a written processor agreement between the Reception and Integration Agency (RIA) and Aramark, breaching Article 28 GDPR principles of controller‚Äìprocessor responsibility.

Findings and Outcome

Both entities failed to demonstrate accountability or implement risk-based technical and organisational measures. The DPC concluded that unclear roles, lack of encryption, and missing audit trails directly violated data-minimisation and integrity principles. Although the footage‚Äôs external transmission could not be proven, the systemic absence of policy controls and documentation was itself deemed a GDPR violation.

Recommended Remediation (as Information Security Manager)

  • Formalise Data-Processing Agreements (DPAs) defining controller and processor duties.
  • Adopt role-based access control (RBAC), CCTV encryption, and secure transfer protocols.
  • Implement incident-response playbooks and subject-access workflows to meet statutory timelines.
  • Provide GDPR awareness training to all staff and contractors handling personal data.
  • Schedule periodic internal audits and data-flow mapping to verify ongoing compliance.

Reflection

This case reinforced the importance of aligning everyday operational practices with formal standards. It demonstrated that compliance is not only legal adherence but a foundation for trust, accountability, and organisational resilience. The exercise also deepened understanding of how frameworks like GDPR intersect with ISO 27001‚Äôs continuous-improvement model to strengthen governance maturity.

Initial Post ‚Äì Pragadheesh Marimuthu (7 October 2025)

Over the past few weeks, I joined this discussion later than I initially planned because of other coursework and professional tasks. The delay, however, allowed me to go through the material from Units 7 to 9 with a clearer understanding of how quantitative risk models are applied in cybersecurity. Reading Spring et al. (2021) was an eye-opener for me, as it changed the way I looked at vulnerability scoring systems such as the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS). I realised that the scoring process is not as objective or reliable as I once believed, and that a lot of interpretation and context are involved in how risks are rated.

Before reading the article, I assumed CVSS was a universal, trustworthy method of quantifying software vulnerabilities. However, Spring et al. (2021) highlight several weaknesses in the system. They point out that CVSS simplifies a wide range of technical details and business impacts into a single number, which can lead to misunderstandings and inaccurate prioritisation. The authors also note that CVSS depends heavily on human judgment, meaning that two analysts can assign different scores for the same vulnerability (Allodi and Massacci, 2014). This makes the results inconsistent and sometimes unreliable for decision-making in large organisations.

Another major problem is that CVSS does not evolve over time. Once a vulnerability is scored, the number remains static even when new exploits appear or patches are released (Gallon, 2019). In today‚Äôs fast-paced IT environments, this limitation can cause organisations to rely on outdated information when making important security decisions.

I agree with the critique presented by Spring et al. (2021). Although CVSS can be a useful starting point, it often gives a false sense of accuracy. Studies such as those by Sabottke, Suciu and Dumitra»ô (2015) show that there is little correlation between CVSS scores and how often vulnerabilities are actually exploited in the real world. This means that an organisation may focus on vulnerabilities that look serious on paper while missing those that pose real business risks.

Among the alternatives discussed, I find the Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorisation (SSVC) method much more practical. It allows different groups such as vendors and asset owners to make risk-based decisions using tailored decision trees (Spring, Hatleback and Householder, 2021). SSVC also fits well with ISO/IEC 27005 principles of continuous risk assessment (Aven and Thekdi, 2024), making it a more flexible and transparent way to prioritise vulnerabilities.

üìä Conceptual Monte Carlo Model Summary

Although no separate artefact files were produced for this unit, the exercise conceptually modelled a risk scenario using Monte Carlo simulation principles. The objective was to estimate the probability of on-time project delivery under variable uncertainty factors.

The model defined three core variables:

  • Success rate (SR): 0.85 ‚Äì 0.95
  • Workload delay (WD): 0.03 ‚Äì 0.07
  • Technical disruption (TD): normally distributed (Œº = 0.20, œÉ = 0.05)

The resulting On-Time Delivery (OTD) metric was derived using the equation:
OTD = SR √ó (1 ‚àí WD) √ó (1 ‚àí TD)

A hypothetical simulation of 10,000 runs would produce a mean OTD around 0.73 ‚Äì 0.78, indicating moderate operational reliability with noticeable sensitivity to technical disruption.
