Unit 1: Human Factors and Cyber Security

Foundations
Focus: Human behaviour, breach causes, insider threats Mode: Lecturecast + reading + case study

Unit 1 introduced the wider role of human factors in cyber security. It positioned human behaviour as a major determinant of success or failure in information systems, especially as organisations increasingly rely on connected systems, cloud platforms, and IoT technologies.

The unit examined how security failures are not always caused by technical weakness alone. Human limitations, social engineering, poor awareness, insider threats, accidental insider behaviour, and organisational pressures can all increase cyber risk.

Key takeaways
  • Humans are often described as the “weakest link”, but this must be understood in context rather than as simple blame.
  • Human capabilities and limitations should influence how security systems are designed.
  • Insider threats include both malicious insiders and accidental insiders who create risk through mistakes or poor judgement.
Representative artefacts
  • Case study review on social engineering and phishing resilience.
  • Initial notes on how human behaviour shapes organisational cyber risk.

Unit 2: Human Behaviour and Security Implications

Behaviour analysis
Focus: Human limitations, user groups, behaviour patterns Seminar: Human behaviours and system security

Unit 2 built on the introductory concepts by analysing how human behaviour, capabilities, limitations, and user-group patterns affect security. The unit focused on how security solutions should be designed with realistic user behaviour in mind.

The formative essay outline activity helped prepare the structure for the Unit 3 essay by encouraging early identification of critical human factors and their implications in a local start-up context.

Key takeaways
  • Security design must account for cognitive limits, stress, workload, and user decision-making patterns.
  • Different user groups may create different kinds of risk depending on role, access level, and behaviour.
  • Understanding behaviour is necessary before designing usable and effective security controls.
Representative artefacts
  • Essay outline preparation for the Unit 3 individual essay.
  • Peer discussion and feedback on Unit 1 case study summaries.

Unit 3: Mental Models and Privacy by Design

Essay assessment
Focus: Mental models, privacy by design, accessibility Assessment link: Individual Essay

Unit 3 introduced mental models, privacy by design, privacy by default, contextual design, and accessibility within usable security. It showed how users and designers may understand the same system differently, creating misalignment between system intention and user behaviour.

The individual essay required a critical analysis of three human factors that a local start-up should consider as part of its IT security strategy. The essay focused on analysis and implications, not solutions.

Essay focus
  • Cognitive overload and decision fatigue.
  • Misaligned mental models between users and security systems.
  • Behavioural disengagement in privacy and data practices.
Key takeaways
  • Mental models shape how users interpret system behaviour and security prompts.
  • Privacy by design and by default reduce reliance on users making repeated complex privacy decisions.
  • Accessibility is part of usable security because inaccessible controls can create exclusion and risky workarounds.
Representative artefacts
  • Individual Essay: A Critical Analysis of Human Factors Influencing Cyber Security Risk in a Local Start-Up Organisation.
  • Turnitin submission record and essay posting preparation for peer review.

Unit 4: Usable Security Design by Default

Peer review
Focus: Usable design planning, privacy by default, case study evaluation Assessment link: Peer Review Submission

Unit 4 focused on planning usable security design projects. It explored how design methods, privacy by design principles, and contextual design approaches can be applied to practical security problems.

The peer review assessment required posting the Unit 3 essay for review and providing constructive feedback on two peer essays. This developed critical evaluation, academic tone, and the ability to identify strengths, weaknesses, and possible measures in human-factor security analysis.

Peer review themes
  • Social engineering susceptibility and cognitive bias.
  • Unintentional insider threats and security fatigue.
  • Socio-technical framing of security risks in start-up environments.
  • Need for constructive, specific, and academically grounded feedback.

Unit 5: Ethics and Organisational Issues

Security culture
Focus: Organisational culture, ethics, legal requirements, GenAI Activity: Presentation outline

Unit 5 moved from individual users to organisational and ethical issues. It examined how culture, leadership, behavioural models, mental models, legal requirements, and ethical considerations influence usable security design.

The unit also considered emerging issues, including how the adoption of generative AI affects organisational procedures and cyber security expectations. The presentation outline activity prepared the structure for the final assessment.

Key themes
  • Positive security culture depends on organisational norms, leadership behaviour, and reinforcement.
  • Mental models can be used as risk communication tools to explain security issues more clearly.
  • Ethical security design must balance protection with autonomy, transparency, and proportionality.
  • Generative AI introduces new challenges around procedure, accountability, user behaviour, and trust.
Representative artefacts
  • Individual Presentation Outline: Designing Usable and Ethical Security Strategies for a Local Start-Up.
  • Notes on security culture, ethical monitoring, and privacy-aware design.

Unit 6: Behaviour Analysis for Positive Security and Risk Communication

Final presentation
Focus: Behavioural models, metaphors, risk communication Assessment link: Individual Presentation

Unit 6 focused on using behavioural models, mental models, and metaphors to develop effective cyber security risk communication strategies. It connected the earlier human-factor analysis with practical strategies for improving organisational security culture.

The final assessment required a 10-minute recorded presentation with transcript. The presentation developed solutions to the human-factor issues identified in the Unit 3 essay, including social and ethical considerations.

Presentation strategy
  • Reduce cognitive overload through security-by-default configuration and alert prioritisation.
  • Align user mental models through contextual onboarding and clearer security prompts.
  • Re-engage privacy practices through privacy-by-design, data minimisation, and meaningful consent.
  • Build sustainable security culture through leadership, social reinforcement, nudging, and ethical governance.
Representative artefacts
  • Final PowerPoint presentation.
  • Transcript and citations supporting the oral presentation.

Module Reflection

From technical security to socio-technical security

The Human Factor module helped me move beyond a purely technical view of cyber security. Across Units 1 to 6, the module showed that cyber risk often emerges from the interaction between people, technology, organisational pressure, culture, and system design.

The most important learning point for me was that users should not simply be blamed as the “weakest link”. In many cases, poor security behaviour is shaped by cognitive overload, unclear system feedback, confusing privacy choices, weak organisational culture, or controls that do not match how people actually work.

The assessments created a clear progression. The essay analysed the human factors that create risk; the peer review developed my ability to evaluate other arguments critically; and the final presentation required me to propose realistic, ethical, and usable security strategies for a local start-up context.

This module is also relevant to my blue-team and SOC career direction. SOC analysts regularly deal with phishing, insider risk, user behaviour, alert fatigue, access misuse, and suspicious activity. Understanding the human side of security helps me interpret incidents more realistically and avoid treating every alert as a purely technical event.
